Wealth Inequality in America

Social Implications

As recent as 2020, the top 1% of households in the United States held fifteen times more wealth than the bottom 50% of households combined. Jeff Bezos, the richest man in America, makes $3,715 per second while 57% of Americans are ONE unexpected $500 expense away from falling into debt and 54% are living paycheck-to-paycheck. If we make billionaires pay their fair share in taxes and contribute back into society, perhaps we can reduce poverty in America and allow those bottom 95% of Americans better benefits such as healthcare and housing.

Economic Implications

The economic implications of having a small majority of Americans holding a majority of the wealth are huge in a way that affects everybody else in America everyday without their knowledge. If the wealthy minority has too much money, then the rest of society is left without the resources they need to engage in the economy, which leads to monopolies and economic stagnation. If we keep allowing this to happen, the 54% of Americans who are living paycheck-to-paycheck will become even less secure than they already are.

Political Implications

Not only is society and the economy at risk by appeasing the ultra-wealthy in their endeavours, but our democracy is at risk as well, and some may even argue it has already been lost. Corruption in the government shows its face in many ways, and billionaires are wearing all of them. Investopedia in 2020 wrote that the billionaire class has abused labor through insufficient pay and work conditions and they have lobbied the federal government for decades to not raise the minimum wage with inflation. Unless we as a people put pressure on the government in a meaningful way to combat this kind of behavior, the government will continue to sacrifice society’s and the economy’s well-being for this wealthy minority.

General Implications

We have seen a trend that the needs of the middle and working class in America has consistently been getting pushed to the wayside in favor of ensuring a bright future for the ultra-wealthy in America. Most of the nations aggregate income is going towards wealthy households with the amount of aggregate income going to the middle-class dropping from 61% to 51% since 1971 (a drop of 19%) and the amount of aggregate income going to the upper-class rose 29% to 48%. The same 19%. This is a clear regression in economic equality, one that is hurting our society, our economy, and our government.
