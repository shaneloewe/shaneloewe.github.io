Wealth Inequality on the Rise in US

by Shane Loewe

Right now, the political climate in America is tense. Amidst the global pandemic, political polarization is at an all time high and it is difficult for us as a society to agree on anything, let alone our government leaders agreeing with each other on anything. Although, despite the polarization, there is one thing that is sure to scare any American, or 95% of Americans, rather. Income inequality in America has been rising steadily since the 1980’s and doesn’t show any signs of slowing down, this means that as working and middle class household income is decreasing or remaining stagnant, the wealthy keep getting wealthier. What are the implications of this?

Social Implications

As recent as 2020, the top 1% of households in the United States held fifteen times more wealth than the bottom 50% of households combined. Jeff Bezos, the richest man in America, makes $3,715 per second while 57% of Americans are ONE unexpected $500 expense away from falling into debt. Yet wildly, most of us aren’t thinking about this in our day-to-day lives, but perhaps we should: and not just working and middle class individuals, but the upper-working class as well.

If you make a $100,000 salary in the US, you are barely in the 50th percentile of wealth distribution. The wealth increase from the 50th to 80th percentile is about $400,000. But for perspective, the increase from the 80th to the 100th percentile is about $10,000,000. It is the ultra-wealthy billionaires, those households in the 5th quintile, the 1% in this country that are going to continue to use their money to generate more money, a luxury those with less expendable or investable income don’t have. If we make billionaires pay their fair share in taxes and contribute back into society, perhaps we can reduce poverty in America and allow those bottom 95% of Americans better access to benefits such as healthcare and housing.

Economic Implications

The economic implications of having a small majority of Americans holding a majority of the wealth are huge in a way that affects everybody else in America everyday without their knowledge. You will hear some classic economists argue that a concentration of wealth in a minority of the population allows for economic growth, that if a small minority of people own a large sum of the wealth, they can make decisions to invest large sums into other companies as well as grow their own company which creates jobs and leads to economic stimulation. However, at what point do the detriments of having a wealthy

minority outweigh the benefits? If the wealthy minority has too much money, then the rest of society is left without the resources they need to engage in the economy, which leads to monopolies and economic stagnation. If we keep allowing this to happen, the 54% of Americans who are living paycheck-to-paycheck will become even less secure than they already are.

Political Implications

Not only is society and the economy at risk by appeasing the ultra-wealthy in their endeavours, but our democracy is at risk as well, and some may even argue it has already been lost. Corruption in the government shows its face in many ways, and billionaires are wearing all of them. Investopedia in 2020 wrote that the billionaire class has abused labor through insufficient pay and work conditions and they have lobbied the federal government for decades to not raise the minimum wage with inflation. This has caused Americans to be unable to support themselves on minimum wage like they used to. Unless we as a people put pressure on the government in a meaningful way to combat this kind of behavior, the government will continue to sacrifice society’s and the economy’s well-being for this wealthy minority.

Conclusion

We have seen a trend that the needs of the middle and working class in America has consistently been getting pushed to the wayside in favor of ensuring a bright future for the ultra-wealthy in America. Most of the nations aggregate income is going towards wealthy households with the amount of aggregate income going to the middle-class dropping from 61% to 51% since 1971 (a drop of 19%) and the amount of aggregate income going to the upper-class rose 29% to 48%. The same 19%. This is a clear regression in economic equality, one that is hurting our society, our economy, and our government.

Shane Loewe
