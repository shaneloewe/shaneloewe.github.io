Wealth Inequality on the Rise in US

by Shane Loewe

Right now, the political climate in America is tense. Amidst the global pandemic, political polarization is at an all time high and it is difficult for us as a society to agree on anything, let alone our government leaders agreeing with each other on anything. Although, despite the polarization, there is one thing that is sure to scare any American, or 95% of Americans, rather. Income inequality in America has been rising steadily since the 1980’s and doesn’t show any signs of slowing down, this means that as working and middle class household income is decreasing or remaining stagnant, the wealthy keep getting wealthier. What are the implications of this?

Social Implications

As recent as 2020, the top 1% of households in the United States held fifteen times more wealth than the bottom 50% of households combined. Jeff Bezos, the richest man

in America, makes $3,715 per second while 57% of Americans are ONE unexpected $500 expense away from falling into debt. Yet wildly, most of us aren’t thinking about this in our day-to-day lives, but perhaps we should: and not just working and middle class individuals, but the upper-working class as well. If you make a $100,000 salary in the US, you are barely in the 50th percentile of wealth distribution. The wealth increase from the 50th to 80th percentile is about $400,000. But for perspective, the increase from the 80th to the 100th percentile is about $10,000,000. It is the ultra-wealthy billionaires, those households in the 5th quintile, the 1% in this country that are going to continue to use their money to generate more money, a luxury those with less expendable or investable income don’t have. If we make billionaires pay their fair share in taxes and contribute back into society, perhaps we can reduce poverty in America and allow those bottom 95% of Americans better access to benefits such as healthcare and housing.

Economic Implications

The economic implications of having a small majority of Americans holding a majority of the wealth are huge in a way that affects everybody else in America everyday without their knowledge. You will hear some classic economists argue that a concentration of wealth in a minority of the population allows for economic growth, that if a small minority of people own a large sum of the wealth, they can make decisions to invest large sums into other companies as well as grow their own company which creates jobs and leads to economic stimulation. However, at what point do the detriments of having a wealthy minority outweigh the benefits? If the wealthy minority has too much money, then the rest of society is left without the resources they need to engage in the economy, which leads to monopolies and economic stagnation. If we keep allowing this to happen, the 54% of Americans who are living paycheck-to-paycheck will become even less secure than they already are.
